02 — Problem

The problem, sharply.

Three pains, one customer. Each pain alone supports a product. The three together support a category.

Pain 1 — The missed-applicant haemorrhage. The owner of a 12-person HVAC shop in Austin gets seven applications in two days from her Indeed post. By the time she sees them on Friday night, four have already accepted offers elsewhere. The remaining three are unreachable. She posts again Monday. The cycle repeats every quarter. Industry-wide, 60–80% of inbound applicant calls go unreturned past four hours. Every applicant lost costs $25–50k in downstream training and lost productivity.

Pain 2 — The Spanish-speaker filter. The single largest available supply pool — foreign-born or non-English-fluent skilled workers — is systematically rejected by US ATS systems trained on English résumé conventions. A 20-year electrician certified in Nicaragua starts the application worse than a novice. The owner doesn't know they're filtering out their best supply.

Pain 3 — Decisions shipped untested. The same owner just rewrote her job post with ChatGPT. She'll roll out a new shift policy next month. She'll send a comp-update email in Q3. Every one of those is now AI-drafted — and shipped without ever asking what the workers will think. There is no "spell-check" for workforce communication. There is no Litmus moment for HR decisions.

The world today

Frontline workforce decisions ship on instinct. Job posts, comp letters, RIF announcements, shift changes, training programs — all increasingly AI-drafted, none validated against any audience model. Operators learn what worked by watching retention and apply rates a quarter later, when it's too late to fix.

The world we build

Every workforce decision audience-tested in 60 seconds. A draft job post returns a predicted apply rate, the top three concerns a Texas HVAC tech persona would raise, and a rewrite that lifts predicted retention by 14%. Calibrated by real-world outcome data. Compounding. Inevitable.

05 — The Playbook

Solo-founder launch playbook.

No co-founder. No employees. No outside capital required to get to default-alive. The whole thing runs from one laptop and twelve SaaS subscriptions.

The exact problem you are solving (so you can recite it in your sleep)

"You posted three HVAC tech jobs this month. You got 41 applicants. You called back 6. You hired 1 — and he ghosted on day three. You're not a recruiter. You're a plumber, an electrician, an HVAC owner trying to run a business. FlexForce calls every applicant within 60 seconds in their language, screens them on the questions a master tech would ask, verifies their license, books your interview, and emails you a one-page brief on each one. You hire faster, lose fewer to ghosting, and never miss the Spanish-speaking 15-year veteran with no résumé. $299 a month. Live tomorrow."

The wedge motion — five things in this exact order

  1. Pick one trade, one state. HVAC in Texas. Highest urgent-hire volume (87k+ active national postings), no state income tax for the founder, the Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation has a clean searchable license database, and ~10,000 licensed shops in the 3–30 employee band. Don't go national until you own this slice.
  2. Build the screening question bank by stealing from masters. Pay three master HVAC techs $500 each to spend 90 minutes answering: "What ten questions would you ask in the first 15 minutes to tell whether someone can actually do this job?" That recording is your trade-specific moat. Compete on this depth, not on UI.
  3. Ship v0.5 to ten design partners at $99/mo for 60 days. Don't free-tier. Charge real money to validate willingness-to-pay. Pick partners with a mix of trade focus, geography (urban + rural), and ownership type (founder-led + GM-led).
  4. Win the workflow before the integration. Don't waste week one on ServiceTitan integrations. Win the standalone product first. Integrations are pulled by paying customers, not pushed by hopeful founders.
  5. Replace yourself with AI ops. Build the customer-onboarding flow, the screening calibration loop, the support triage, and the cold-outbound personalization as your own internal AI workflows from day one. You are the COO of an AI-staffed company. Treat it that way.

07 — The Bigger Play

GigWise.ai — the audience layer for AI-driven workforce decisions.

Same shape as your Litmus thesis. Different surface. Bigger ceiling. The wedge product creates the calibration data; the platform monetizes the dataset.

The reframe

The Litmus argument applied to creative AI was: every AI-generated artifact that will meet a real human currently ships without an audience reality-check. The exact same gap exists in workforce decisions — and the buyer pool is 10× larger, the regulatory pressure is faster-building, and there is no analog of Litmus yet attempting it.

Job posts. Comp letters. Shift-change announcements. RIF emails. Training scripts. Onboarding sequences. Benefit-change comms. Performance-review templates. Every one of these is increasingly AI-drafted in 2026 — and every one ships without ever asking what the actual frontline workers will think.

GigWise.ai is the validation layer. Not a marketplace. Not a survey tool. A Litmus-shaped orchestration + calibration + dataset business — applied to the deskless workforce.

What it does, in one paragraph

A web app, a Slack add-on, and a Chrome extension. Paste your draft — a job post, a shift-change email, a comp-update letter, a training script. Pick a workforce persona (HVAC tech in TX, CNA in FL, warehouse worker in OH, retail associate in CA, immigrant tradesperson cohort). In sixty seconds get back: a predicted apply / engagement / retention impact score, the top three concerns that persona would raise, a rewrite that improves the prediction, an inclusivity-and-bias flag set (English fluency, foreign credentials, demographic exposure), and a compliance check against EEOC, state-specific labor law, and AI-decision disclosure rules. Every result logged for audit. Calibrated against real-world apply and retention outcomes piped in from FlexForce.ai.

08 — Why Not

Why not a marketplace.

The original GigWise framing was an on-demand skilled-trades marketplace. That instinct is correct on demand but wrong on shape. Here is why.

Why marketplaces fail for a solo founderWhy audience layer wins
Two-sided liquidity — need workers and buyers simultaneously, in the same geography, at the same time. Cold-start problem is brutal. One-sided product — buyer pastes draft, gets answer. No supply side to develop.
Compliance burden carried by the platform — license verification, insurance, W-9, workers' comp. Each state has different rules. No labor performed — GigWise predicts and rewrites; it does not deploy workers. No labor-law exposure.
Margin compression toward 12–15% — Workrise, Instawork, BlueCrew all converge here. Brutal unit economics at sub-scale. SaaS gross margins 85%+, plus high-margin dataset licensing at scale.
Capital-intensive — payroll float, supply incentives, geographic build-out. Solo founder cannot win this. Capital-light — every dollar goes to model + integrations + content, not float or supply subsidies.
Existing well-funded competitors — Workrise ($300M+), Instawork ($200M+), Trade Hounds, Wonolo. Race-to-the-bottom on take rates. No incumbent in workforce-decision audience validation. First product to ship owns the category narrative.
The data is transactional — "this job got filled" — and not particularly licensable. The data is predictive — "this language predicts 9% higher retention for foreign-credentialed HVAC techs in TX" — and licensable for years.
The strategic point: A marketplace would have been a five-year slog to $20M ARR with rising CAC, narrow margins, and a defensive moat. The audience-layer play is a five-year arc to three credible business lines — workflow SaaS, certification standard, proprietary dataset — sitting on the same engineering core, with compounding empirical moat. Same domain. Different shape. Much bigger ceiling.

12 — Risks

Pressure test — what kills this.

Risk 1: VeroSkills moves down-market. Mitigation — vertical depth wins. VeroSkills has a generalist screening AI; FlexForce has trade-specific question banks calibrated against retention data per state. At $1M ARR with a 3-trade × 10-state footprint, the data moat is structurally unreachable in their cost of acquisition.

Risk 2: ServiceTitan or Jobber ships first-party AI recruiting. Mitigation — get on their marketplaces fast. FSM platforms favor partner-built apps that drive seat upgrades and don't cannibalize their own customer-comms revenue. Offer Jobber a white-label option by month 18.

Risk 3: Voice AI commoditizes; OpenAI ships a "Hire Agent" template. Mitigation — the moat is not the voice model. It is: (a) workflow-deep integrations with FSM stacks, (b) per-state license-board plumbing, (c) trade-specific screening data calibrated to retention outcomes, (d) embedded distribution. None of that is a model-layer thing.

Risk 4: EEOC / AI-bias regulation tightens around hiring AI. Mitigation — build NYC AEDT-style 4/5ths-rule audit reporting from day one. Default to "human-in-loop" on offers. Stay on the right side of California AB-2930 and the federal AI EO framework from launch. This becomes a feature, not a liability.

Risk 5: Macro hiring slowdown in trades. Mitigation — even at 30% lower volume, the demographic cliff is structural. Trades hiring is countercyclical to white-collar AI displacement. The product gets more important when hiring gets harder, not less.

Risk 6: Tradespeople refuse AI screening. Mitigation — published 2025–26 data shows 83%+ candidate-positive rate on AI phone screens. Bilingual is a feature, not a bug. Keep calls under 5 minutes. Offer human-callback option visibly.

Risk 7: GigWise.ai data flywheel never reaches escape velocity. Mitigation — even without FlexForce ground truth, GigWise.ai has standalone unit economics at the workflow-SaaS layer. The dataset is upside, not the floor. If the data network never compounds, the workflow product still pays for itself.
